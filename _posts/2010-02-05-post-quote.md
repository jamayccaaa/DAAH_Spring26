# Extra Credit Assignment 1: Love Data Week

## Human Subjects Research with REDCap and NYU Research Integrity and Compliance
### February 13, 2026, 7:00 - 8:30 PM, NYU NY


<p>Last February 13, 2026, in celebration of the Love Data Week, I participated in an online talk hosted by New York University titled “Human Subjects Research with REDCap and NYU Research Integrity and Compliance,” together with members of the Research Integrity and Compliance and Data Services teams. The session was part of their efforts to promote responsible data practices and to highlight the importance of proper data management, especially in research involving human participants. It was an informative and well-organized discussion that brought together experts who shared their knowledge and experiences in supporting ethical and secure research processes.

In summary, the talk gave a detailed overview of what counts as human subjects research and the responsibilities that come with it, especially in terms of protecting participants. I learned that research involves a living individual when data is collected through intervention, interaction (such as interviews, surveys, or online communication), or identifiable private information. Research is defined as a systematic investigation meant to contribute to generalizable knowledge, so it’s important to be clear about both what we’re collecting and my intent. The session also emphasized that if data is collected from another country, we need to follow that country’s data protection regulations. They mentioned that NYU’s Human Research Protection Program (HRPP) and Institutional Review Board (IRB) play a key role in reviewing and overseeing studies to make sure risks are minimized, and participant selection is equitable, informed consent is obtained when appropriate, and privacy and confidentiality are protected, especially for vulnerable populations.

Other than that, the talk also focused on the IRB process and what a strong application should include. Submissions are completed through Cayuse and should clearly detail data collection methods, interview or survey materials, any observations or recordings (with consent), interventions or manipulations, and plans for data storage and confidentiality. A major point was to focus less on the type of review and more on completing the application accurately and thoughtfully, while also planning for review timelines and having a faculty sponsor check the project. Consent was described as an ongoing process, and it’s important to not promise anonymity if the study only ensures confidentiality. The speaker covered situations like consent waivers, letters of collaboration when there are power dynamics, disclosure of any AI tools used, and the importance of properly classifying data so it can be protected effectively. Overall, the talk emphasized being intentional, transparent, and responsible at every stage of working with human subjects. 

The presentation shared with us different links to check out that introduce the IRB and the Human Research Protection Program. Which i do think is very helpful to people who don't really know about this resource. 

The talk focused on acquiring data responsibly and making sure that the information we use is accurate and reliable before completing any type of research. I think this connects to our work in Introduction to Digital Humanities because it highlights the importance of not immediately trusting the data or content we receive without verifying or fact-checking it first. One activity we’ve done where this applies is the digital narrative task, where our class asked different AI models to respond to the same prompt. Since AI-generated content can sometimes be inaccurate or misleading, this talk reinforced that it’s important to double-check the information before using the information or simply believing it. It made me more aware that even when data is easy to access, there is still a responsibility to review it carefully and think critically about its reliability.

My main takeaway from the talk is to be mindful of how important it is to be careful and intentional when doing research. I think this will be especially useful in classes that involve research, where the accuracy and credibility of data really matter. I also see this being very helpful for capstone projects, since strong research requires careful data collection, ethical considerations, and proper handling of information. Being able to know that resources like the IRB and the HRPP exist made me less worried about creating research on my own. Knowing that there are resources available and help that I can ask about my study and the credibility of my study. Overall, this topic reminded me that taking the time to verify and manage data properly makes research more credible, responsible, and effective, which is something I will carry into my future academic work and eventually into professional practice.
</p>
