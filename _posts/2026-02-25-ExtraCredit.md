## Human Subjects Research with REDCap and NYU Research Integrity and Compliance [Ready For Grading]
> February 13, 2026, 7:00 - 8:30 PM, NYU NY

<p>Last February 13, 2026, in celebration of Love Data Week, I participated in an online talk hosted at New York University Campus titled “Human Subjects Research with REDCap and NYU Research Integrity and Compliance,” together with members of the Research Integrity and Compliance and Data Services teams. The session was part of their effort to promote responsible data practices and to highlight the importance of proper data management, especially for research involving human participants. It was an informative and well-organized discussion that brought together experts who shared their knowledge and experiences in supporting ethical, secure, and well-managed research processes. Overall, the talk focused on helping researchers understand their responsibilities when working with data and human subjects. </p>

<p>
The session began with a clear explanation of what qualifies as human subjects research and the responsibilities that come with it, particularly in protecting participants. Research involves a living individual when data is collected through intervention or interaction, such as interviews, surveys, observations, or online communication, or when it involves identifiable private information. The speakers said that researchers must be intentional and transparent about both what they are collecting and the purpose behind their study. They also emphasized that if data is collected from another country, researchers must follow that country’s data protection regulations, which adds another layer of responsibility when conducting international or cross-cultural research. </p>

<p> A central part of this responsibility is the role of oversight offices such as NYU’s Human Research Protection Program (HRPP) and Institutional Review Board (IRB). The HRPP promotes a research environment that respects the rights and welfare of individuals who participate in research and ensures the university’s compliance with policies and regulations. The IRB, on the other hand, reviews research protocols to make sure studies are ethically sound and that participants’ privacy and confidentiality are protected. Their goal is to minimize risk, ensure fair and unbiased participant selection, and protect vulnerable populations. They also check whether proper procedures are in place to maintain confidentiality and safeguard collected data. </p>

<p>One of the most important themes of the session was consent, which was presented as a major part of ethical research. The speakers emphasized that consent should not be treated as a one-time form or a simple requirement to complete. Instead, it should be understood as an ongoing process that respects the participant’s autonomy throughout the study. Participants should clearly understand what the research involves, how their information will be used, and what risks or benefits may be involved. Researchers were also reminded to be honest about the level of privacy they can provide and not to promise anonymity if the study only guarantees confidentiality. This distinction is important because anonymity means identities cannot be traced at all, while confidentiality means identities are protected but still accessible to the research team under secure conditions. </p>

<p> The discussion also covered situations where consent requirements may vary, such as consent waivers, and emphasized the importance of careful justification in such cases. In studies where power dynamics may exist, such as research conducted within institutions or workplaces, letters of collaboration may also be required to ensure ethical participation. To make it short, consent is not just a procedural step but a responsibility that reflects respect for participants and their rights.</p>

<p>Another major focus of the talk was the IRB application process and what makes a strong submission. Applications are completed through Cayuse, a system that NYUAD uses to record and review IRB protocols. A strong application should clearly describe data collection methods, interview or survey instruments, observations or recordings with consent, any interventions or manipulations, and plans for data storage and confidentiality. One important point from the speakers was to focus less on the type of review and more on making sure the application is complete, accurate, and carefully prepared. They also shared practical tips from the HRPP, explained each step of the review process, and emphasized the importance of planning ahead for review timelines. Having a faculty sponsor review the project before submission was also recommended to avoid delays and improve the quality of the proposal.</p>

In addition, the speakers highlighted the importance of properly classifying data so that it can be protected according to its level of sensitivity. They also mentioned the need to disclose any AI tools used in the research process, reinforcing the broader theme of transparency and accountability. Throughout the discussion, the overall message was to be intentional, careful, and responsible at every stage of working with human subjects, from planning and data collection to storage and reporting. Another key theme of the talk was the importance of acquiring data responsibly and making sure that the information used in research is accurate and reliable. This connects to our work in Introduction to Digital Humanities because it emphasizes the need to avoid immediately trusting data or content without verifying or fact-checking it. One activity where this applies is the digital narrative task, where our class asked different AI models to respond to the same prompt. Since AI-generated content can sometimes be inaccurate or misleading, the session reinforced the importance of reviewing information carefully before using it or accepting it as true. It made me more aware that even when data is easy to access, there is still a responsibility to evaluate it critically and consider its reliability.</p>

<p>The presentation also provided links and resources (links below) that introduce the IRB and the Human Research Protection Program. I think this is especially helpful for people who may not be familiar with these offices or who are unsure where to start when planning a study. They also showed a decision chart that helps determine whether a project requires an IRB application. Having access to these materials makes the process feel more manageable and gives researchers a clearer understanding of the support available to them.</p>

<p>My main takeaway from the talk is the importance of being careful, mindful, and intentional when conducting research. A huge part of this responsibility is respecting participants through proper consent and clear communication about how their data will be used and protected. I believe the information from the session is especially useful in classes that involve research or data, where accuracy, ethics, and credibility are very important. I can also see how this will be helpful for future capstone projects, since strong research depends on careful data collection, ethical considerations, and proper handling of information. Knowing that resources like the IRB and HRPP exist also made me feel less worried about conducting research on my own in the future. It is reassuring to know that there are offices and professionals who can provide guidance and answer questions about study design, ethics, and research credibility. Overall, the session reminded me that taking the time to verify information, obtain meaningful consent, and manage data responsibly makes research more credible, respectful, and effective. This is something I will carry into my future academic work and eventually into my professional practice.</p>
